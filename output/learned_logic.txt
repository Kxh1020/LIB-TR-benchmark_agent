## 1) Question Stem Construction (how to frame the scenario)

1. **Anchor the stem in a concrete test context.**
   - Specify abuse type (nail penetration, indentation, bending, overcharge, external heating, reduced pressure, confined space).
   - Specify cell format/chemistry (21700/18650/pouch/prismatic; LFP/NCM811/etc.).

2. **State controlled variables explicitly and keep them few.**
   - Example controls: SOC fixed, heating power fixed, same chamber setup, same geometry.
   - Mention what is varied (pressure, indenter shape, penetration mode, squeeze force, PCM type).

3. **Give observational channels but not conclusions.**
   - Mention measured signals (voltage fluctuations, surface vs internal temperature, pressure peaks, venting, gas composition, force–indentation curve stages, imaging).
   - Avoid telling the mechanism; let options propose interpretations.

4. **Use “engineering reasoning” prompts when data are partial.**
   - If stem lacks numbers, explicitly invite “based on fundamental electro-thermal / transport / combustion / fire dynamics principles”.

5. **Define operational criteria/thresholds inside the stem.**
   - Provide a criterion like “TR when self-heating rate > X” or “failure threshold defined by voltage loss after rest and no TR”.

6. **Include at least one asymmetry that blocks pattern-matching.**
   - Examples: internal vs surface temperature mismatch; repeated ISC pulses that self-terminate; semi-confined vs open space; axial vs radial penetration.

7. **Use multi-select stems frequently for coupled phenomena.**
   - Reserve single-choice for one key causal link (e.g., dominant heat source, strongest interface).

---

## 2) Evidence-to-Question Transformation (turning facts into reasoning tasks)

8. **Convert observations into “supported vs not supported” claims.**
   - Ask “consistent with findings/engineering reasoning” rather than “true”.

9. **Force constraint satisfaction across multiple statements.**
   - Each option should touch a different constraint (electrical current, resistance evolution, heat transfer, chemistry, mechanics, sensing fidelity).

10. **Ask about directionality/trends, not raw facts.**
   - Monotonic vs non-monotonic relations (pressure vs flame height, squeeze force vs surface temperature peak, SOC vs onset temperature).

11. **Translate mechanisms into measurable proxies.**
   - Example: “RISC below threshold ⇒ TR possible”; “CID activation ⇒ stepwise voltage drop”; “inflection point ⇒ irreversible damage onset”.

12. **Use comparative reasoning between methods/configurations.**
   - Compare triggering methods (heating plate/rod/coil), geometries (axial/radial), environments (air/N2; OS/SCS), materials (PCM types, cathode chemistries).

13. **Embed a “what explains X?” subtask in option set.**
   - Provide a phenomenon (double pressure peak, current drop, voltage fluctuation) and have options propose competing explanations.

14. **For chemistry items, filter by “directly produces species Y”.**
   - Ask to select reactions that directly generate H₂/CO₂/CO/CH₄/C₂H₄, explicitly excluding side categories (radical termination, gas-phase oxidation).

## 3) Distractor Design Archetypes (plausible wrong options)

15. **Overgeneralization distractors.**
   - Use “always/inevitably/only/necessarily” to create tempting but falsifiable extremes.

16. **Single-factor fallacy distractors.**
   - Claim one variable dominates (e.g., “penetration depth is the key factor regardless of SOC”, “pressure alone determines risk”).

17. **Condition-violation distractors.**
   - State something generally true but incompatible with the stem’s conditions (e.g., assuming surface temperature equals internal hot spot; assuming forced convection always matters).

18. **Mechanism-confusion distractors.**
   - Mix correct entities with wrong causal direction (e.g., separator melting is endothermic but claimed to directly reduce TR; cathode structure “strengthens” with SOC).

19. **Proxy-misinterpretation distractors.**
   - Treat an instrument readout as a direct proof of a different quantity:
     - MTS increase ⇒ “cell number increased”
     - Highest concentration at vent outlet ⇒ “maximum combustion risk always there”
     - Voltage alone ⇒ “reliably detects every ISC event”

20. **Monotonicity traps.**
   - Offer monotonic claims where the intended reasoning allows peaks/saturation (PCM thickness “always” helps; conductivity “no saturation”; flame height “highest at lowest pressure”).

21. **Terminology distractors in reaction-selection questions.**
   - Include reactions that produce the target gas only indirectly, or require excluded pathways (radical termination, gas-phase oxidation), or are balanced but irrelevant to “primary internal reactions”.

22. **Near-correct distractors (partially true).**
   - One clause correct, second clause wrong; forces careful parsing.

## 4) Reasoning Requirements (what solvers must do)

23. **Causal electro-thermal reasoning.**
   - Link resistance → current → Joule heating → localized temperature rise → TR threshold.

24. **Coupled multiphysics reasoning.**
   - Require interaction of mechanics (rupture/delamination), electrical (ISC type), thermal (heat dissipation), and chemistry (exothermic reactions).

25. **Signal interpretation reasoning.**
   - Decide which signals are early-warning vs lagging (gas vs temperature/pressure), and when surface measurements misrepresent internal state.

26. **Comparative mechanism reasoning.**
   - Same outcome (TR) via different triggers; identify what changes (short development mode) vs what stays (underlying mechanism).

27. **Thermodynamic + kinetic reasoning.**
   - Separate “heat release potential” from “trigger/onset sensitivity” (activation energy trends, heating-rate effects).

28. **Exclusion reasoning (necessary vs sufficient).**
   - E.g., “ISC may be trigger but not main heat source”; “TR can occur without ISC under oxygen release pathways”.

29. **Combustion/fluid reasoning under confinement.**
   - Use ventilation/void volume/pressure effects to reason about accumulation, flammability, deflagration vs sustained combustion.

---

## 5) Difficulty Control (how to tune hardness)

30. **Control difficulty by how much quantitative detail is withheld.**
   - Harder: no numbers; rely on sign/trend/threshold logic.
   - Easier: include explicit threshold definitions (e.g., TR criterion, force threshold, RISC critical).

31. **Increase difficulty via multi-select with mixed truth density.**
   - Include 2–5 correct options; ensure wrong options are not obviously absurd.

32. **Remove keyword cues by distributing vocabulary.**
   - Avoid repeating unique phrases from stem in the correct option only; use shared terminology across options.

33. **Use “paired contrasts” to force discrimination.**
   - Provide two options differing only by one modifier (monotonic vs non-monotonic; internal vs surface; early vs late stage).

34. **Include cross-domain distractors to prevent rote recall.**
   - Example: combine electrochemistry with heat transfer; sensor physics with battery failure; CFD hazard metrics with flammability rules.

35. **Gate single-choice items with a clearly defined target.**
   - Use single-choice when the stem logically implies one dominant mechanism (e.g., which reaction dominates heat; which interface strongest).

36. **Keep options locally plausible by matching the stem’s scale.**
   - If stem emphasizes “early stage”, include distractors about “late-stage dominance” and vice versa.

37. **Add one “meta-method” option in modeling/measurement questions.**
   - E.g., “model fits data ⇒ physically correct” (tempting but wrong), or “screening rule quantifies severity” (overreach).

These rules can be implemented directly in a generator: pick an abuse scenario + controlled variables + observable signals, define a threshold or comparison, then write options spanning correct causal consequences and the distractor archetypes above.
