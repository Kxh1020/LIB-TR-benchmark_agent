1) **Question stem construction (how to write the scenario)**
1. State the **test/abuse mode + cell format** up front (e.g., nail penetration, indentation, thermal abuse; pouch vs cylindrical), so reasoning is conditioned on a concrete context.
2. Include **operating conditions as constraints** (SOC, pressure, heating rate, penetration direction/speed, boundary cooling, confinement/venting) but avoid giving the conclusion those constraints imply.
3. Describe **measured signals/observables** (voltage fluctuation, force–displacement inflection, surface vs internal temperature, gas jet/pressure peaks, mass loss, particle size fractions) in neutral language; do not label them as “warning,” “failure,” or “cause.”
4. When relevant, explicitly name **what is being compared** (two chemistries, three triggering methods, two environments) and keep all other conditions “held constant” to force controlled-comparison reasoning.
5. Define any **threshold/criterion** operationally (e.g., “TR when self-heating rate > X,” “critical failure threshold defined by voltage loss after rest”), enabling inference without external trivia.
6. Use **mechanistic primitives** as allowed givens (e.g., “higher contact resistance limits current,” “lower pressure changes air density/oxygen availability”) but stop short of mapping them to outcomes.
7. Prefer stems that require choosing **multiple correct statements** when the phenomenon is multifactorial; use single-choice when there is a clear “best supported” mechanism.

2) **Evidence-to-question transformation (turn facts into reasoning tasks)**
8. Ask what is **supported/consistent/defensible** given the scenario, not what is universally true in the field.
9. Convert observations into **causal-chain selection** tasks: “Given X is observed and Y is known, which mechanisms/outcomes follow?”
10. Use **constraint satisfaction**: provide 2–4 constraints that jointly eliminate options (e.g., “surface underestimates internal,” “TR only below RISC threshold,” “confinement accumulates gases”).
11. Force **comparative reasoning** by tying each option to a direction-of-change claim (increase/decrease/monotonic/non-monotonic) across a controlled variable.
12. Mix **cross-domain coupling** in the same item (electrical ↔ thermal ↔ mechanical ↔ chemical), so no single fact lookup suffices.
13. When asking about reactions, require mapping from equations to **product identity** (e.g., “directly generates H2/CO2/CO/CH4/O2”), excluding irrelevant reaction classes via instructions.
14. Use “**prior to TR**” vs “during TR” vs “after venting” time windows to require temporal reasoning (precursor vs consequence).

3) **Distractor design archetypes (how to write plausible wrong options)**
15. **Overgeneralization distractors:** use absolutes (“always,” “inevitably,” “dominant in all cases,” “perfectly reversible”) that fail under stated conditions.
16. **Mechanism confusion:** pair a correct phenomenon with the wrong driver (e.g., correct temperature trend but attribute to cathode when it’s interface resistance; correct gas hazard but wrong flammability logic).
17. **Condition-violation distractors:** offer statements that can be true generally but contradict a stem constraint (e.g., claims assuming uniform temperature when stem highlights strong gradients; assumes open space when stem is semi-confined).
18. **Scope expansion:** extrapolate cell-level findings to module/pack-level inevitabilities (or vice versa) without allowing for heat transfer/geometry constraints.
19. **Measurement misinterpretation:** confuse proxies with causes (e.g., “MTS increase proves cell number,” “surface temperature equals internal hotspot,” “voltage alone reliably indicates ISC events”).
20. **Monotonicity traps:** propose linear/monotonic relationships where non-monotonic behavior is plausible under coupled processes (pressure, PCM thickness, SOC vs severity, venting vs combustion).
21. **Single-factor dominance distractors:** claim one variable solely determines the outcome when the stem signals interacting factors (SOC vs penetration depth; ventilation vs void volume).
22. **Numerical exaggeration distractors:** insert hard thresholds or “two orders of magnitude” changes without being supported by the provided constraints.

4) **Reasoning requirements (ensure reasoning over recall)**
23. Require at least one of: (a) **elimination by contradiction** with given constraints, (b) **multi-step causal reasoning**, (c) **trade-off reasoning** (e.g., insulation vs heat dissipation; conductivity vs accumulation), (d) **rate/process reasoning** (kinetics vs thermodynamics).
24. Make correct options depend on **directional logic** (if R increases → I decreases → local heating changes), not on memorized constants.
25. Use **proxy-vs-ground-truth reasoning**: ask whether a measured signal can or cannot faithfully represent an internal state under gradients or time delays.
26. Include at least one option that is **partially true but invalidated by timing/definition** (e.g., true after TR but not “prior to onset”), forcing careful reading and temporal alignment.

5) **Difficulty control (how experts tune challenge)**
27. Provide **enough givens to reason**, but omit one linking inference step; the student must supply it (e.g., connect contact resistance to current to heating).
28. Avoid obvious keyword matches by using **near-synonyms** and by distributing key clues across multiple sentences rather than repeating terms from options.
29. Increase difficulty by adding **interacting variables** (SOC + geometry + environment pressure + heat transfer) while keeping each individually familiar.
30. Use **heterogeneous option types** in the same question (mechanism, measurement validity, trend, boundary-condition effect) to prevent pattern guessing.
31. Calibrate hardness with **plausible distractors that are locally correct** (true in another test mode/chemistry) but wrong under the stem’s specific setup.
32. For multi-select items, include 1–2 options that are clearly wrong, 2–3 that require real reasoning, and 0–1 that are “tempting but subtly invalid,” to create separation without randomness.
