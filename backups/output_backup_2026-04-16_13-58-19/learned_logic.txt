# Question Design Rules for Engineering Reasoning MCQs
## (Battery Safety and Thermal Runaway Domain)

---

## 1. Stem Construction Rules

1. Present a specific experimental setup with defined parameters (geometry, chemistry, loading mode, SOC) before asking for judgment about behavior or mechanism.
2. State observable outcomes (voltage drop, temperature rise, gas generation) without stating their cause; require the solver to identify the mechanism.
3. Define boundary conditions explicitly (e.g., pressure range, force threshold, SOC level) and ask what behavior is admissible within those bounds.
4. Describe a multi-stage physical process and ask which intermediate behaviors are consistent with the described progression.
5. Present a system with two or more interacting variables (e.g., mechanical damage + thermal loading) and ask for judgments that require reasoning about their interaction.
6. Define a measurement technique and its limitations, then ask which conclusions are supportable from that measurement.
7. Describe a comparative experimental design (e.g., two chemistries, two configurations) and ask which differential conclusions are supported.
8. Present partial structural information (e.g., a force-displacement curve with an inflection point) and ask what physical event that feature represents.
9. Describe a failure sequence and ask which statements about its stages are consistent with the described progression.
10. Define a system-level configuration (e.g., module with spacing, PCM, fuses) and ask which component-level behaviors propagate to system-level outcomes.
11. Present a condition-dependent phenomenon and ask which conclusions hold only under the stated conditions, not in general.
12. Describe a protective or mitigating intervention and ask which safety claims are defensible given known mechanism limitations.
13. Present competing physical mechanisms (e.g., thermal vs. mechanical triggering) and ask which dominates under stated conditions.
14. Embed a regime-defining threshold (e.g., critical resistance, critical force) and ask which behaviors occur above vs. below that threshold.
15. State what was measured and what was not measured, then ask which conclusions are within the evidentiary scope.

---

## 2. Evidence Transformation Rules

1. Convert a measured signal (voltage, temperature, pressure) into a question about what physical event that signal does or does not indicate.
2. Transform a force-displacement curve feature into a question about the underlying damage mechanism it reflects.
3. Convert a comparative experimental result (chemistry A vs. chemistry B) into a question about which mechanistic difference is responsible.
4. Turn a sequence of observed events into a question about causal ordering and whether each step is necessary for the next.
5. Convert a parameter sweep result (e.g., varying SOC, force, pressure) into a question about whether the trend is monotonic, threshold-dependent, or non-monotonic.
6. Transform a protection mechanism (PCM, separator coating, flame retardant) into a question about what failure modes it addresses and which it does not.
7. Convert a multi-component system observation into a question about which component's behavior dominates the system response.
8. Turn an experimental observation made under specific conditions into a question about whether that observation generalizes to different conditions.
9. Convert a gas composition measurement into a question about which reactions are consistent with producing that composition.
10. Transform a numerical simulation result into a question about which physical assumptions are necessary for that result to be valid.
11. Convert a thermal abuse test result into a question about which heat generation pathway is the primary contributor under those conditions.
12. Turn a material characterization result (DSC, impedance, SEM) into a question about what safety-relevant inference is and is not supported by that data type.
13. Convert a module-level propagation observation into a question about which cell-level mechanism drives the module-level outcome.
14. Transform a delayed failure observation (failure during cycling after mechanical abuse) into a question about whether the cause is instantaneous or progressive.
15. Convert a safety intervention result into a question about which aspect of the thermal runaway sequence was affected and which was not.

## 3. Distractor Design Rules

**Overgeneralization Distractors**
1. Construct a distractor by taking a result observed under specific conditions (e.g., one SOC, one chemistry) and stating it as a universal rule.
2. Construct a distractor by extending a trend observed in a limited parameter range to the full parameter space.

**Mechanism Confusion Distractors**
3. Construct a distractor that names the correct physical phenomenon but attributes it to an incorrect cause or pathway.
4. Construct a distractor that correctly identifies a reaction product but assigns it to the wrong precursor reaction.
5. Construct a distractor that correctly identifies a thermal event but misidentifies which electrode or component initiates it.

**Condition Violation Distractors**
6. Construct a distractor using a statement that is true in general but violates a constraint explicitly stated in the stem.
7. Construct a distractor that is correct for a different SOC, temperature, or loading regime than the one specified.

**Scope Expansion Distractors**
8. Construct a distractor that infers a system-level safety conclusion from a component-level experimental result.
9. Construct a distractor that recommends a design change based on a single test condition without accounting for regime dependence.

**Numerical Exaggeration Distractors**
10. Construct a distractor that states a correct directional trend but overstates the magnitude (e.g., "linear" when the relationship is nonlinear, "orders of magnitude" when the change is moderate).
11. Construct a distractor that asserts a threshold value or critical parameter without sufficient basis in the stated evidence.

**Ignoring Boundary Conditions Distractors**
12. Construct a distractor that is valid only below or above a critical threshold but is stated without that qualification.
13. Construct a distractor that ignores a regime transition (e.g., from low to high pressure, from mechanical to thermal dominance) that changes the governing mechanism.

**Causal Overclaim Distractors**
14. Construct a distractor that interprets a correlation between two observed variables as a direct causal relationship.
15. Construct a distractor that attributes a complex multi-factor outcome to a single identified variable.

**Ignoring Multi-factor Interaction Distractors**
16. Construct a distractor that assumes single-variable control of a system known to have multiple interacting variables.
17. Construct a distractor that ignores the coupling between thermal, mechanical, and electrochemical processes.

**Measurement Scope Distractors**
18. Construct a distractor that claims a surface measurement (temperature, voltage) accurately reflects an internal state that was not directly measured.
19. Construct a distractor that treats an indirect indicator as a definitive diagnostic of a specific internal event.

**Protective Mechanism Overclaim Distractors**
20. Construct a distractor that claims a safety intervention prevents a failure mode it can only delay or partially mitigate.

## 4. Reasoning Enforcement Rules

**Causal Chain Reasoning**
1. Require the solver to trace a multi-step physical sequence (e.g., mechanical damage → contact resistance change → current change → temperature change) to evaluate a conclusion.
2. Require the solver to identify which step in a causal chain is the rate-limiting or dominant step under stated conditions.

**Constraint-Based Reasoning**
3. Require the solver to check whether a stated conclusion is consistent with all constraints defined in the stem simultaneously.
4. Require the solver to identify which conclusions are eliminated by a specific constraint (e.g., a defined threshold, a defined measurement limitation).

**Boundary and Admissibility Reasoning**
5. Require the solver to determine whether a conclusion is admissible given the evidence, rather than whether it is possible in principle.
6. Require the solver to distinguish between what the evidence supports and what it merely does not contradict.

**Regime-Dependent Reasoning**
7. Require the solver to recognize that the correct answer depends on which regime (low/high SOC, low/high pressure, low/high force) the system is in.
8. Require the solver to identify when a behavior changes character across a threshold rather than varying continuously.

**Scale Transfer Reasoning**
9. Require the solver to evaluate whether a component-level result is sufficient to justify a system-level conclusion.
10. Require the solver to identify when a local measurement (surface temperature, surface voltage) cannot be extrapolated to an internal state.

**System Interaction Reasoning**
11. Require the solver to account for coupling between two or more physical domains (thermal-mechanical, electrochemical-thermal) when evaluating a conclusion.
12. Require the solver to identify which interaction dominates under specified conditions.

**Elimination via Contradiction**
13. Require the solver to eliminate options by identifying a direct contradiction with a stated condition or measured result.
14. Require the solver to identify which options require an assumption not provided or supported by the stem.

**Failure Mode and Safety Reasoning**
15. Require the solver to distinguish between conditions that trigger failure, conditions that accelerate failure, and conditions that are merely correlated with failure.
16. Require the solver to evaluate whether a safety intervention addresses the primary failure mechanism or only a secondary one.

**Mechanism Competition Reasoning**
17. Require the solver to identify which of two competing mechanisms dominates under the stated conditions.
18. Require the solver to recognize when a mechanism that is dominant in one regime becomes secondary in another.

**Propagation Reasoning**
19. Require the solver to evaluate whether a local failure event will propagate to neighboring components given the described system configuration.
20. Require the solver to identify which system parameter (spacing, conductivity, ventilation) controls propagation rate or extent.

---

## 5. Difficulty Control Rules

**Incomplete but Sufficient Information**
1. Provide enough information to support exactly one correct conclusion, but withhold information that would make the answer obvious by keyword matching.
2. Include one variable that is irrelevant to the correct answer to require the solver to identify which parameters are controlling.

**Avoiding Keyword Matching**
3. Phrase correct options using mechanistic language rather than repeating terminology from the stem.
4. Phrase plausible distractors using terminology from the stem to attract solvers who rely on surface pattern matching.

**Partially Correct Options**
5. Include options that are correct in one part but contain an unsupported or incorrect second claim joined by a connective (e.g., "because," "therefore," "and thus").
6. Include options where the direction of a trend is correct but the functional form (linear, monotonic, threshold) is incorrect.

**Boundary Judgment Forcing**
7. Place the correct answer at a regime boundary where the behavior is counterintuitive relative to the general trend.
8. Include an option that is correct in the general case but incorrect under the specific conditions stated in the stem.

**Requiring Elimination Instead of Recognition**
9. Design the correct option set so that it cannot be identified by recognizing a single correct statement; require elimination of multiple plausible distractors.
10. Include at least one distractor that is experimentally plausible and mechanistically coherent but contradicts a specific stated condition.

**Counterintuitive Correct Options**
11. Include a correct option that contradicts a commonly held simplification (e.g., stronger protection increases risk, larger nail reduces temperature rise).
12. Include a correct option that involves a non-monotonic relationship where a monotonic one might be expected.

**Multi-Step Reasoning Requirement**
13. Design questions so that the correct answer requires at least two sequential inferential steps from the stated evidence.
14. Avoid questions answerable by a single direct lookup of a fact; require the solver to combine two or more pieces of information.

**Qualitative Rather Than Numerical Reasoning**
15. Frame questions in terms of trends, thresholds, and admissibility rather than numerical calculation to prevent formula-based shortcuts.
16. When numerical values are included, ensure they serve as regime indicators rather than calculation inputs.

**Competing Mechanism Difficulty**
17. Include two options that each invoke a real and relevant mechanism, where only one is dominant under the stated conditions.
18. Include an option that correctly identifies a secondary mechanism as if it were the primary one.

**System vs. Component Distinction**
19. Include a distractor that correctly describes component behavior but incorrectly applies it to system-level behavior.
20. Require the solver to identify the level of analysis (component, cell, module, pack) at which a conclusion is valid.

**Unsupported Extrapolation Detection**
21. Include a distractor that makes a recommendation or prediction beyond the scope of the stated experimental conditions.
22. Require the solver to identify when a conclusion requires an assumption that was not stated or justified in the stem.
