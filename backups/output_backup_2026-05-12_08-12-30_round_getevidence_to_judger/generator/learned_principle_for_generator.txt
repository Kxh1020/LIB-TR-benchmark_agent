1. Stem Construction Rules
1. Specify operating conditions, test configuration, and measurement scope explicitly before asking for conclusions.
2. Frame the stem around a defined threshold, boundary condition, or transition point to anchor reasoning.
3. Combine two interacting variables (e.g., pressure and chemistry, SOC and loading mode) so single-factor reasoning fails.

2. Evidence Transformation Rules
1. Extract a counterintuitive finding from source data and convert it into a plausible-sounding wrong option.
2. Convert a local experimental observation into a candidate statement that tests whether it generalizes beyond its scope.
3. Split a multi-step causal chain across options so each option covers only one link, requiring full-chain reasoning.

3. Distractor Design Rules
1. Create one distractor by reversing the direction of a reported trend while keeping all other details accurate.
2. Create one distractor by attributing a correct mechanism to the wrong component, phase, or location.
3. Create one distractor by extending a conditionally true finding into an unconditional universal claim.

4. Reasoning Enforcement Rules
1. Require simultaneous evaluation of multiple correct options so elimination of one wrong answer is insufficient.
2. Include at least one option that is true in isolation but contradicts a stated boundary condition in the stem.
3. Embed a negation qualifier (e.g., "which are wrong") in some stems to force careful logical polarity checking.

5. Difficulty Control Rules
1. Increase difficulty by adding a mechanistically plausible distractor that requires domain calculation or inequality reasoning to reject.
2. Reduce difficulty by limiting options to clearly distinct mechanisms with no overlapping surface language.
3. Control difficulty by varying how many correct answers exist: two-correct sets are easier; four-or-more-correct sets are harder.
