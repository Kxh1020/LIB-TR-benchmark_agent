## Stem Construction Rules
1. Start with a realistic engineering scenario (test method/abuse mode/operating condition) and specify what is being evaluated (safety outcome, failure threshold, propagation, model validity).
2. Provide **observations and constraints** (signals measured, boundary conditions, what is held constant) without interpreting them.
3. Include at least **two interacting factors** (e.g., mechanical + thermal, geometry + SOC, confinement + ventilation) so single-variable reasoning is insufficient.
4. Define the **regime** explicitly (early vs late stage, pre-failure vs post-failure, low vs high loading/pressure) and ask conclusions limited to that regime.
5. Use **admissibility wording** (“supported/consistent/defensible/physically reasonable/wrong”) rather than “true/false” to force evidence-limited judgment.
6. Specify **system boundaries** (cell vs module vs pack; internal vs surface measurements; local interface vs global response).
7. Provide a **decision criterion** or operational definition (e.g., failure threshold defined by a signal after rest; TR defined by a rate criterion) so answers must align with definitions.
8. Omit the key causal link or mechanism and require the solver to infer it from the given constraints and principles.
9. When comparing methods/configurations, state “all else equal” controls to prevent trivial attribution to uncontrolled variables.
10. For multi-select items, ensure the stem implies **multiple statements can simultaneously hold** (e.g., coupled mechanisms or stage-dependent behaviors).

## Evidence Transformation Rules
1. Convert measured signals/trends into questions about **what can be concluded** (not what happened), using limits of observability (e.g., surface vs internal sensing).
2. Turn “parameter changes” into **threshold/monotonicity/peak** admissibility tasks (e.g., whether effects must be monotonic, whether a critical value exists).
3. Use partial evidence to ask for **non-implication**: which conclusions are *not justified* even if they sound plausible.
4. Transform comparative setups into **mechanism discrimination**: ask whether differences arise from development path vs fundamental mechanism.
5. Map component-level phenomena to system-level judgments only when justified; otherwise ask which options **overstep scale transfer**.
6. Use “defined failure criteria” to test whether statements respect the **metric** (e.g., voltage-loss criterion vs thermal runaway occurrence).
7. Convert “observed staging” (multi-stage curves/processes) into reasoning about **state transitions** and what can/cannot be inferred at inflection points.
8. Turn coupled hazards (thermal + mechanical + electrical) into **dominance/regime** reasoning: which mechanism dominates under which condition range.
9. When chemistry is involved, ask for **thermo-kinetic defensibility** (activation energy trends, heating-rate effects) rather than naming reactions from memory.
10. For modeling/validation contexts, convert “fits data” into questions about **identifiability and physical completeness**, not accuracy alone.

## Distractor Design Rules
1. **Overgeneralization distractor:** claim a variable “always” increases/decreases risk or response, ignoring non-monotonic or regime-limited behavior.
2. **Scope expansion distractor:** infer pack/module behavior directly from a single-cell observation (or vice versa) without a transfer pathway.
3. **Mechanism confusion distractor:** describe the right phenomenon but assign the wrong driver (electrical vs thermal vs mechanical vs transport limitation).
4. **Condition-violation distractor:** use a statement true in another regime (e.g., steady-state, low rate, open environment) but invalid under stated constraints.
5. **Boundary-condition ignoring distractor:** neglect stated controls (constant SOC, fixed heating power, identical environment) and attribute effects to them anyway.
6. **Correlation→causation distractor:** treat an indicator signal as proof of an internal event rather than a proxy with limitations.
7. **Local-to-global measurement distractor:** assume externally measured quantities accurately represent internal maxima/minima.
8. **Model-validity fallacy distractor:** claim “multiple models fit data ⇒ physically correct” or “calibration ⇒ completeness.”
9. **Single-factor simplification distractor:** ignore multi-factor interaction and attribute outcomes to one variable despite coupled mechanisms.
10. **Engineering recommendation leap distractor:** jump from limited lab evidence to a universal design recommendation without acknowledging constraints/trade-offs.

## Reasoning Enforcement Rules
1. Require at least one **multi-step chain**: condition → intermediate mechanism/state → observable → admissible conclusion.
2. Force **elimination reasoning** by making several options partially true but wrong due to scope, regime, or definition mismatch.
3. Make at least one correct option rely on **measurement limitation reasoning** (what sensors can/cannot prove).
4. Include **regime-switch reasoning** (low vs high load/pressure; pre-TR vs TR; confined vs open) so answers depend on conditions.
5. Enforce **scale-transfer reasoning** (interface ↔ layer ↔ cell ↔ module) with explicit boundaries so unjustified extrapolation is penalized.
6. Use **competing mechanisms** in the stem so the solver must decide which dominates under the stated constraints.
7. Require **consistency with defined criteria** (TR definition, failure threshold, “supported” means not exceeding evidence).
8. In method-comparison questions, require reasoning about **controlled variables** and what differences can legitimately be attributed to.

## Difficulty Control Rules
1. Provide information that is **incomplete but sufficient**, so solving depends on principles and constraints rather than missing facts.
2. Avoid keyword-matching by using **near-synonymous phrasing** across options and embedding the real discriminator in conditions/definitions.
3. Mix **global and local descriptors** (e.g., global curve vs local damage) so the solver must reconcile levels of description.
4. Include distractors that are **correct in general** but invalid under the stated regime or measurement boundary.
5. Increase difficulty by adding **one extra interacting variable** (e.g., pressure + chemistry + venting) rather than adding calculations.
6. Control difficulty with **option granularity**: make options differ by scope words (always/only/primarily/may), thresholds, or stage qualifiers.
7. Use multi-select format when the scenario naturally supports **multiple simultaneous truths**, raising the need for careful admissibility checks.
8. Limit numerical cues; when numbers exist, use them as **threshold markers** rather than computation triggers.
9. Add at least one option that is **counterintuitive but defensible**, forcing principle-based evaluation over intuition.
10. In modeling questions, increase difficulty by testing **identifiability vs fit**, and **stress-state coverage vs single-test calibration**.
